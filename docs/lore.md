# Server Lore — The Fall of Olympus

## The World Before

The gods ruled from Olympus for millennia. Immortal, untouchable, eternal. Their power flowed through every corner of creation — Demeter's warmth in every harvest, Hephaestus' fire in every forge, the crash of Triton's waves against every shore. The mortal world below carried on unaware, sustained by divine forces it never saw.

Then something woke up.

The gods do not speak its name. They refer to it only in fragments — *the storm that does not end*, *the shadow beneath the mountain*, *the hunger that devours divinity*. Whatever it is, it is older than Olympus. Older than the Titans. It slept for ages beyond counting, and now it is awake, and it is angry.

It struck Olympus without warning. The mountain cracked. The divine halls burned with a fire that consumed immortality itself. Gods who had never known fear learned it in an instant. Their power — the essence of what made them divine — began to shatter like glass, scattering across worlds in fragments too small to reclaim alone.

They fled.

One by one, diminished and desperate, the gods fell into the mortal world. Not as conquerors. Not as rulers. As refugees.

---

## The Arrival

They did not arrive together. Each god landed where their nature pulled them — Demeter in the fields, Hephaestus near stone and fire, Heracles where conflict already raged. They arrived weakened, their divine essence fractured into pieces scattered across the world. Each god carries only a fraction of what they once were.

Promachos arrived first. He is not a god — he is a demigod, a herald, a messenger sent ahead to prepare. His task was simple: find mortals. Test them. See if any were worthy of what would come next.

Because the gods realized something terrible in their flight. Whatever hunts them — it feeds on divine power. Every time a god acts directly, every time they wield what remains of their strength, it draws the enemy closer. They cannot fight. They cannot rebuild alone. They cannot even open their own vaults.

They need mortals.

---

## Why Mortals Matter

Mortal effort is invisible to the enemy. When a farmer harvests wheat, when a smith smelts iron, when a warrior cuts down a beast — that labor carries no divine signature. It is beneath notice. And yet, when that labor is performed in a god's name, devoted to their cause, it generates something extraordinary: a resonance that can restore fractured divine essence without alerting the thing that hunts them.

This is the secret of the emblem system. Players are not simply doing tasks — they are performing acts of devotion that slowly reassemble the shattered power of the gods. Every crop harvested for Demeter rekindles a spark of her warmth. Every ore smelted for Hephaestus fans the dying embers of his forge. Every creature slain for Heracles echoes the hero's ancient strength.

The three components of each emblem are literal fragments of a god's shattered essence. When all three are gathered and the ceremony performed, the god regains enough strength to recognize the mortal as a true champion — and to trust them with something more.

---

## The Vaults

When the gods fled Olympus, they carried what they could — sealed vaults containing artifacts, weapons, treasures, and relics accumulated over millennia. These vaults are all that remains of Olympus' glory.

But the enemy was clever. As the gods fled, it cursed the locks. Divine hands cannot open them. No god can touch their own treasure. The vaults can only be unsealed by keys forged from mortal devotion — the physical manifestation of labor performed in a god's name.

The tier system reflects how deep into the vault a key can reach:

- **MORTAL** — Surface-level contents. Trinkets. Scraps the gods barely care about.
- **HEROIC** — Useful artifacts. Things a mortal champion might need.
- **LEGENDARY** — Weapons and relics of genuine power. The gods notice when these emerge.
- **MYTHIC** — Items that once sat in the halls of Olympus itself.
- **OLYMPIAN** — The deepest treasures. Things the gods brought because they could not bear to leave them behind. Blessings, fragments of divine craftsmanship, enchanted golden apples from Hera's garden.

Each god guards their vault jealously — not out of greed, but out of fear. If the wrong thing falls into mortal hands, or if too much divine energy is released at once, it could draw attention. The gods ration what they give, watching, measuring, hoping.

---

## The Hidden Vaults

Some gods walked among mortals long before the fall, disguised under different names. Demeter became *Ceres*. Hephaestus became *Vulcan*. Heracles became *Mars* in the stories told by different peoples. Triton was whispered of as *Neptune's heir*. Charon was feared as *Dis*, lord of passage.

Under these names, they hid secondary vaults — smaller, more personal collections unknown even to the other gods. These Roman-aspect vaults contain the gods' most guarded possessions: unique artifacts that carry fragments of identity rather than raw power.

Only those who have fully proven themselves — who have assembled an emblem and demonstrated absolute loyalty — can even learn these vaults exist. The gods reveal them reluctantly, with a mix of gratitude and vulnerability. To open a Roman vault is to see a god's private self, the face they wore when they chose to be close to mortals rather than above them.

## The Gods

### Promachos — The Herald

Promachos is not an Olympian. He is a demigod — half-divine, half-mortal — and that duality is precisely why he was chosen as the herald. He can act where the gods cannot. He can speak freely, move openly, test and recruit without drawing the enemy's gaze.

He arrived before the others, tasked with a single mission: prepare the mortal world. Find those with potential. Organize them. When the gods began arriving — broken, afraid, barely recognizable — Promachos was the one who gave them structure. He established the emblem system, designed the framework of trials, and serves as the intermediary between gods too proud or too weakened to ask for help directly.

Promachos does not fully understand the threat. He knows enough to be afraid. He knows the gods are hiding something from him — something about what destroyed Olympus that they refuse to share even with their own herald. This gnaws at him. He serves faithfully, but there are moments — brief flickers in his dialogue — where his frustration shows.

He believes in mortals more than the gods do. He has to. He is half-mortal himself.

---

### Demeter — Goddess of the Harvest

*Emblem color: Gold. Arrived in the overworld's fields.*

Demeter landed softly. Where the other gods crashed and burned, Demeter settled into the earth like a seed falling into soil. She was drawn to the farmlands, the places where mortals already worked the land, and she felt a kinship she had never expected.

Of all the gods, Demeter lost the most personal thing. Not power — not weapons or relics. She lost her *warmth*. The essence that made crops grow, that turned barren soil fertile, that brought spring after every winter. It shattered when Olympus fell, and now she can feel it — scattered across the world in three fragments, each one tied to a different aspect of the harvest.

She is the most approachable of the gods. Grateful, genuine, startled by her own vulnerability. She never needed mortals before. Now she watches them work the fields and wonders how she ever overlooked them. There is a sadness in Demeter that she hides behind warmth — she remembers Persephone, her daughter, and wonders if the underworld still stands, or if the enemy has consumed that too.

When she speaks of why she fled, her voice goes quiet: *"The fields of Olympus burned. Not with fire — with absence. The soil turned to nothing. Not barren. Nothing. As if it had never existed at all."*

**What she knows about the enemy:** Demeter felt it in the earth before the others felt it at all. A tremor. A wrongness in the roots of the world. She tried to warn them. They did not listen until it was too late. She does not describe the enemy as a creature — she describes it as an *un-growing*. A force that reverses creation.

---

### Hephaestus — God of the Forge

*Emblem color: Dark gray. Arrived near stone and lava.*

Hephaestus did not land gracefully. He fell — as he always has. The god of the forge has been thrown from high places before, and each time he has dragged himself upright, found raw materials, and built something from the wreckage.

This time is different. His forge — the divine forge of Olympus, where he crafted weapons for gods and armor for heroes — is gone. Not destroyed. *Unmade*. The enemy didn't break it; it erased the concept of it. Hephaestus can remember every detail of the forge, but when he reaches for the memory of how it worked, there is only static.

He set up a new forge in the mortal world. It is crude. It sputters. The fire burns ordinary orange instead of divine white. But it works, barely, and that is enough for Hephaestus. He has always been the god who cares more about function than beauty.

He does not speak much. He respects action over words. When mortals bring him ore, smelt iron, build with their hands — he watches with an expression that is not quite pride but something close. He remembers a time when he made things for gods who never thanked him. These mortals thank him. He doesn't know what to do with that.

**What he knows about the enemy:** Hephaestus built the locks on Olympus' gates. He built them to be unbreakable. *They broke in seconds.* Whatever came through those gates was not merely powerful — it operated on principles his craft could not account for. He describes the enemy not as a force but as an *impossibility*. Something that should not exist according to the rules of creation. This terrifies him more than raw power ever could.

---

### Heracles — Hero-God of Valor

*Emblem color: Red. Arrived where conflict already raged.*

Heracles is not like the others. He was born mortal. He earned his divinity through twelve impossible labors, through suffering and loss and sheer refusal to die. When the other gods fled Olympus in terror, Heracles was the last to leave — not because he was brave enough to fight, but because he was stubborn enough to try.

He failed. Whatever attacked Olympus swatted him aside like a mortal child. The hero who strangled the Nemean Lion, who held the sky on his shoulders, who dragged Cerberus from the underworld with his bare hands — was nothing to it. This humiliation burns in him still.

Of all the gods, Heracles understands mortals best, because he was one. He knows what it means to face something stronger than you and fight anyway. He knows what it means to be afraid and to act regardless. When he looks at the players who take up his emblem, he does not see servants or tools. He sees himself, centuries ago, before divinity softened his edges.

He is blunt. He does not dress his words in poetry. He says what he means and means what he says. If a mortal earns his respect, he will tell them so. If they disappoint him, he will tell them that too.

**What he knows about the enemy:** Heracles fought it. Briefly, uselessly, but he fought it. He is the only god who can describe what it felt like to strike it — and have the blow *mean nothing*. Not deflected. Not absorbed. Simply... irrelevant. *"I have fought monsters my entire life. This was not a monster. Monsters can be killed. This was something else."* He will not say more. But sometimes, when he thinks no one is watching, he flexes his hands as if checking they still work.

---

### Triton — God of the Sea

*Emblem color: Dark aqua. Retreated to the ocean depths.*

Triton did not flee to the mortal world — he retreated into it. When Olympus fell, he dove into the deepest ocean trench he could find and sealed himself away. The sea has always been his fortress, his kingdom, the one domain he ruled without challenge. If the enemy wanted him, it would have to drown first.

He waited. Weeks. Months. Listening to the currents for signs of pursuit. When none came, he surfaced — barely — and found a world of mortals who had never heard of him. This offended him.

Triton is proud. Arrogant, even. He is the son of Poseidon, heir to the seas, and the indignity of needing mortal help physically pains him. He treats players with barely concealed contempt — until they earn his emblem. Then, reluctantly, his tone shifts. Not to warmth. Never warmth. But to something approaching respect. *"You are... less disappointing than expected."*

He did not come to the surface willingly. He came because he felt Demeter's warmth fading from the world, felt Hephaestus' forge sputtering, and realized that whatever destroyed Olympus was not content to stop there. The sea would not protect him forever. If the other gods fell entirely, the enemy would turn its gaze downward eventually.

He needs mortals. He would rather choke on brine than admit it.

**What he knows about the enemy:** Triton felt the ocean *flinch*. Not a wave, not a current — the entire ocean recoiled from something. He has never felt that before. The sea fears nothing. The sea *is* fear, for most creatures. But something made the deepest water pull back, and in that moment, Triton understood that nowhere was safe. *"My father's trident could split the earth. Whatever this is... the earth would not even notice."*

---

### Charon — Ferryman of the Dead

*Emblem color: Dark purple. Emerged from the nether.*

Charon is not an Olympian. He never was. He is older than Olympus, older than the Titans, a figure from the primordial dark who existed before the gods organized creation into something orderly. He ferried the dead across the River Styx when the concept of death was new, and he has ferried them ever since.

He did not flee Olympus because he was never there. He fled the *underworld* — his own domain, his own river, the crossing he has maintained since before memory. Something entered the underworld from below. Not from above, where the gods fell — from *below*. From somewhere even Charon did not know existed. And it began unmaking the dead.

Not killing them — they were already dead. *Unmaking* them. Erasing them from the river. Souls that had crossed centuries ago simply ceased to exist, and the Styx — the eternal river that had never faltered — began to *dry*.

Charon fled into the nether because it was the closest thing to the underworld the mortal world offered. He is afraid, though he will never say so. His harshness, his dismissiveness, the contempt he shows to the living — it is armor. The ferryman of the dead is afraid of what death has become.

He demands two emblems before he will speak to mortals not because of pride — but because he needs to know they are strong enough to hear what he has to say without breaking.

**What he knows about the enemy:** More than any other god. And he shares less than any other god. Charon saw what came from below. He watched it consume souls that were under his protection. He carries that guilt. When he speaks of it — rarely, in fragments — his voice drops: *"I have ferried the dead for eternity. The dead trusted me. The river trusted me. I failed them."* He pauses. *"Do not ask me what I saw. You are not ready. None of you are ready."*

---

## The Divine Armor — Rite of Investiture

When a mortal unlocks a god's emblem, something changes. The god no longer sees a servant or a volunteer — they see a *potential champion*. And with that recognition comes a terrible question: is this mortal strong enough for what comes next?

The divine armor quest is not a reward. It is a preparation. The gods know — even if they will not say it plainly — that something must eventually be confronted. Divine power alone cannot defeat it. Mortal strength alone cannot survive it. But a mortal wrapped in divine essence, powered by mortal will, carrying a god's trust? That is something new. Something the enemy has not encountered before.

Each god approaches the rite differently, but the structure is the same:

1. The god asks the mortal to perform an act of service — not fetching items, but *doing something* that directly helps the god reclaim lost power or restore something broken
2. Through the quest, the god reveals fragments of their personal experience — what they lost, what they fear, what they remember of the fall
3. Upon completion, the god bestows their gift — a fragment of remaining divine essence given freely, an act of profound trust
4. The mortal forges the armor themselves, combining divine gift with mortal-crafted diamond, creating something that belongs fully to neither world

The armor is soulbound not by magic but by nature. It is attuned to the mortal who forged it. It cannot be worn by another. It cannot be discarded. It is a part of them now, as much as the god's trust is a part of them.

---

### Demeter's Rite — Restoring the Grove

*"There was a grove on Olympus where nothing ever died. The first seeds I ever planted still grew there. When I fled... I felt them wither behind me. But one root survived. I carried it in my hands all the way down. It is dying. I need you to save it."*

Demeter asks the champion to restore a sacred grove — to cultivate life in conditions that resist it. This is not farming. This is fighting entropy. The tasks involve nurturing growth where growth should not be possible, coaxing life back into soil that has forgotten how to sustain it.

Through the quest, Demeter reveals her grief. Not for Olympus — for her garden. For the simple, quiet things that died when the mountain fell. She speaks of Persephone, her daughter, and wonders aloud if the underworld still exists. She does not cry. Gods do not cry. But her voice breaks.

Upon completion, she gives the champion four **Golden Seeds** — not literal seeds, but crystallized fragments of her divine warmth. The last sparks of the grove that grew on Olympus.

*"These are all I have left of home. I am trusting you with my memory. Do not let it fade."*

---

### Hephaestus' Rite — Reigniting the Forge

*"My forge is dying. Not this one — this one was never truly alive. The real forge. The divine forge. Its fire still exists, scattered, cooling, fading. If I lose it entirely, I will never build anything that matters again. I need you to bring the heat back."*

Hephaestus asks the champion to perform acts of creation and destruction that generate enough heat — enough raw transformative energy — to reignite fragments of his divine forge fire. This means smelting at scale, breaking down and rebuilding, pushing the craft to its limits.

Through the quest, Hephaestus reveals vulnerability he shows no one else. He speaks of the things he built on Olympus — Achilles' armor, Athena's shield, the automata that guarded the divine halls. All unmade. All erased. He describes the sound they made as they ceased to exist: *"Not breaking. Not crumbling. Just... silence. Where there had been my work, there was silence."*

Upon completion, he gives the champion four **Embers** — fragments of the original forge fire, still burning after all this time, sustained by nothing but Hephaestus' stubborn refusal to let them die.

*"I built things for gods who never thanked me. You... you helped me when I asked. That is worth more than gratitude. Take these. They are the last heat of Olympus."*

---

### Heracles' Rite — The Trial of Will

*"I have fought everything. Everything. And I lost. For the first time in my existence, I was nothing. I need to know that my strength wasn't wasted — that what I learned in all those battles can live in someone else. I need you to prove that a mortal can stand where a god fell."*

Heracles does not ask for help restoring power. He asks the champion to *survive*. To face escalating combat challenges that test not just strength but endurance, adaptability, and the refusal to quit. This is Heracles passing on his legacy — the twelve labors distilled into a mortal frame.

Through the quest, Heracles reveals what it felt like to be mortal — and what it felt like to lose that mortality. He speaks of his labors with honesty, not glory. The fear he felt. The times he almost died. *"The songs make it sound heroic. It wasn't heroic. It was terrifying, and I did it anyway, because the alternative was worse."* He tells the champion that this is the only quality that matters: not strength, not skill, but the willingness to act when every instinct says to run.

Upon completion, he gives the champion four **Sigils** — the mark of Heracles, the same symbol that was burned into his own mortal flesh when he ascended to godhood. He has never given it to another.

*"When I was mortal, I wished for someone to fight beside me. I never found them. Maybe you're what I was looking for. Take my sigil. Carry it where I cannot."*

---

### Triton's Rite — Reclaiming the Depths

*"The ocean is forgetting me. Currents I commanded for millennia now flow without my voice. Creatures I ruled swim past as if I am nothing. My dominion is dissolving. I need you to remind the sea who I am."*

Triton asks the champion to reassert his authority over the ocean — to clear desecrated monuments, to command the tides through action where Triton can no longer command them through will. This is not exploration. It is *reconquest*.

Through the quest, Triton's pride cracks. He admits, in fragments, that the sea was never truly his — it was his father's, Poseidon's, and Poseidon is gone. Not dead. *Gone*. Triton does not know where. He does not know if Poseidon fled somewhere deeper, or if the enemy consumed him entirely. The ocean's forgetting of Triton might simply be the sea mourning its true master.

This is the wound Triton hides behind arrogance: the possibility that he was never enough.

Upon completion, he gives the champion four **Pearls** — formed in the deepest trench, in absolute darkness, under pressure that would crush anything mortal. They are the ocean's memory of Triton's authority, crystallized before that memory fades completely.

*"I have never given anything willingly. The sea takes. It does not give. But you... you have reminded me that even the tide must turn. Take these. They are the last proof that I mattered."*

---

### Charon's Rite — Mending the River

*"The river is broken. The Styx — the boundary between life and death, the crossing that has held since the first mortal drew breath — is fractured. Souls are slipping through. Not crossing. Not arriving. Simply... lost. I need you to seal the fractures before the river forgets what it is."*

Charon asks the champion to perform acts in the nether that restore the boundary between worlds — actions that reinforce the concept of death itself. Charon does not want death to end. Death is necessary. Death is the river. Without the river, there is no crossing, and without the crossing, there is no peace for the dead.

Through the quest, Charon reveals the most. And says the least. He speaks in fragments. Broken sentences. Long pauses. He describes the underworld before the fall with a tenderness that contradicts everything about his harsh exterior. *"It was quiet. The river moved slowly. The dead were... patient. They trusted me to take them across. Every single one."* He stops. *"I do not know where they went. The ones who vanished. I do not know if they are suffering."*

This is the heaviest lore. Charon carries guilt the other gods do not. He was responsible for the dead, and he could not protect them. His rite is not about restoring his power — it is about atonement.

Upon completion, he gives the champion four **Obols** — the ancient coins placed on the eyes of the dead to pay the ferryman. These are not ordinary coins. They are the first obols ever given, by the first mortal who ever died. Charon has kept them for eternity. He gives them away now because he believes the champion will need them more than he will.

*"The first mortal to die placed these coins on their own eyes. They did not know what death was. They only knew they trusted whoever waited on the other side. I was there. I took them across. I have carried these ever since."*

He pauses.

*"If what I fear is coming... you will need passage that even death cannot deny. Take them. And when the time comes... I will be waiting at the river."*

## What the Gods Will Not Say

Every god knows more than they reveal. Every god carries a piece of the full picture. But none will share it all — not yet. Not until they trust their champions completely. Not until the armor is forged, the vaults are opened, and the mortal world has proven it can bear the weight of what is coming.

The players will learn the truth in fragments. A word here. A name almost spoken. A flinch when certain questions are asked. The gods are protecting their champions — or perhaps protecting themselves — from the full horror of what drove them from their home.

The truth will come. Eventually. When the gods decide their champions are ready.

Or when they run out of time.

## Lore Fragments — What Gods Reveal During Quests

These are moments of storytelling woven into quest progression. Not all at once — drip-fed as the champion advances through each stage of the rite. Players who complete all five armor sets will hold pieces of a larger puzzle that only makes sense when compared.

**Demeter (early quest):** *"The other gods think I am naive. Perhaps I am. But I remember the first wheat that ever grew. I remember how the mortal who planted it wept with joy. That is worth protecting. That is worth everything."*

**Demeter (mid quest):** *"Persephone... she was in the underworld when it happened. Charon will not speak to me about it. I do not know if that means she is safe, or if he cannot bear to tell me otherwise."*

**Demeter (late quest):** *"The ground shook before the sky fell. I felt it in every root, every stem. The earth was trying to warn us. We did not listen. We never listen to the earth."*

**Hephaestus (early quest):** *"I was the first god to be thrown from Olympus. Did you know that? My own mother threw me. I survived then. I will survive now. Surviving is what I do."*

**Hephaestus (mid quest):** *"I built the chains that held Prometheus. I built the box that Pandora opened. I built weapons that ended wars and weapons that started them. Every creation carries consequence. I am beginning to wonder what consequence Olympus itself carried."*

**Hephaestus (late quest):** *"The locks I built for the vaults — they were not just physical. They were conceptual. They locked the idea of the treasure away from the idea of opening. Something... understood that. Something broke the concept, not the lock. I have never encountered anything that thinks like that."*

**Heracles (early quest):** *"The songs say I was fearless. The songs are wrong. I was afraid every single time. Fear is not weakness. Fear is information. It tells you something dangerous is real."*

**Heracles (mid quest):** *"My father is Zeus. Was Zeus. I don't know what he is now. When Olympus fell, the lightning stopped. Not the storms — those are natural. The lightning that carried intention. Zeus' will made manifest. It simply... ceased."*

**Heracles (late quest):** *"I stayed to fight. I was the last one on the mountain. I want you to know that. Not for glory — so you understand that I tried. I threw everything I had. It was like striking the ocean. Not even a ripple."*

**Triton (early quest):** *"My father built the oceans. Every wave was his design. Do you know how it feels to stand in your father's masterwork and realize it no longer recognizes you?"*

**Triton (mid quest):** *"The other gods pity me. I can see it. 'Poor Triton, playing king in an empty sea.' They do not understand. The sea is not empty. It is full of things that no longer obey."*

**Triton (late quest):** *"I found something in the deepest trench. A sound. Not a creature — a sound without a source. It was the ocean screaming. I have never surfaced faster in my life."*

**Charon (early quest):** *"Mortals fear death. I have never understood this. Death was a crossing. A quiet boat ride. I made sure of it. Every soul arrived safely. That was my purpose. My only purpose."*

**Charon (mid quest):** *"The river began flowing backwards. Do you understand what that means? The Styx has flowed one direction since the first death. Downstream. Always downstream. When it reversed... the dead began walking upstream. Back toward life. But they were not alive. They were not dead. They were... wrong."*

**Charon (late quest):** *"I will tell you something I have told no one. When the underworld fractured, I heard a voice from below the river. Not in the water — beneath it. Beneath the concept of the river. A voice that said one word. I will not tell you the word. But I will tell you this: it was not angry. It was not threatening. It was greeting me. As if it had been waiting. As if it knew my name."*

## Connecting the Fragments

Players who pursue multiple armor sets will begin to notice patterns:

- **Demeter** felt it in the earth. **Hephaestus** felt it in the concept of his locks. **Triton** heard it in the deep. **Charon** heard it beneath the underworld. It came from **below** — from underneath everything.
- **Heracles** fought it and describes striking the ocean. **Triton** describes the ocean screaming. **Demeter** describes the earth trying to warn them. The natural world knew before the gods did.
- **Hephaestus** says it broke *concepts*, not objects. **Charon** says it *unmade* souls. **Demeter** says the soil became *nothing, not barren*. It does not destroy — it **erases**.
- **Zeus' lightning stopped.** **Poseidon is gone.** **The underworld fractured.** The three brothers who divided creation — sky, sea, underworld — were the primary targets.
- **Charon heard a voice that knew his name.** Whatever this is, it is intelligent. It has been waiting. And it is not hostile in the way the gods expected. It is something worse: it is *patient*.

No single god holds the full picture. No single player will piece it together alone. The lore is designed to reward those who talk to each other, who compare notes, who realize that five separate stories are actually one story told from five different vantage points.

## The Unanswered Questions

These are deliberately left open. They are hooks for future content, future emblems, future reveals:

- Where is Zeus? Where is Poseidon? Where is Hades? The three most powerful gods are absent. Are they dead? Imprisoned? Fighting somewhere the lesser gods cannot see?
- What did Charon hear? The word spoken from beneath the river. The name. The greeting.
- What is the enemy's goal? It erases. It unmakes. But why? What does it want with creation? Does it want anything at all, or is it simply what it is — an un-making given form?
- What happened to Persephone? Demeter's daughter was in the underworld when it fractured. Charon won't talk about it. Is she alive? Is she the key to something?
- Why does mortal labor go unnoticed by the enemy? Is it truly invisible to it, or is it simply *uninteresting*? What happens when the champions become powerful enough to register?
- The divine armor is preparation. But preparation for what, specifically? The gods speak of confrontation but never details. Do they have a plan? Or are they improvising and hoping?

The story continues when the gods are ready. Or when they run out of time.
